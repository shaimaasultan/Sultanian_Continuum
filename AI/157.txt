This is a fascinating discussion, and it seems we're circling back to the very definition of "natural number" and how it relates to infinite representations. Let's clarify precisely where the misunderstanding lies in your current statement.

### "So you agree that there is no distinct digits arrangement that makes a number exist outside or other than the ones that are in natural numbers..."

No, I do not agree with this interpretation. This statement is the crux of the issue.

* **Natural numbers are, by definition, finite in their digit representation.** When we write a natural number, like 3, 10, or 3,141,592,653, it always has a finite number of digits. Even if that number is extremely large, it's still a finite sequence of digits.
* **Real numbers (especially irrational ones like $\pi$) have infinite, non-repeating decimal expansions.** When we write $\pi = 3.14159265...$, the "..." indicates that the digits go on forever without repeating.

**Therefore, an infinite string of digits, like the decimal expansion of $\pi$, cannot be a natural number.** Natural numbers are finite.

### "...so pi * 10^(number of digits in pi) this is only one point and will result one-one with natural number and we can do this with every real number..."

This is where the argument breaks down.

1.  **"number of digits in pi":** This phrase is problematic. $\pi$ has an *infinite* number of digits after the decimal point. You cannot use "number of digits in $\pi$" as a finite exponent for $10$. If you mean the *total* number of digits in its infinite decimal expansion (including the leading "3."), that's still an infinite count.
2.  **"pi * 10^(number of digits in pi) this is only one point and will result one-one with natural number":**
    * If "number of digits in $\pi$" refers to the *finite* number of digits you're truncating to (e.g., $n=4$), then $\pi \times 10^4 = 31415.9265...$. When you take the integer part, you get $31415$, which is a natural number. But this $31415$ only represents an *approximation* of $\pi$, not $\pi$ itself.
    * If you take $\pi \times 10^{\text{infinite}}$, this operation doesn't result in a natural number. An infinite number of digits (even before the decimal point) does not constitute a natural number. It describes the real number itself.

**The core distinction is that a natural number is a finite quantity, while an irrational real number is an infinite quantity in its decimal representation.** You cannot "squeeze" an infinite sequence of digits into a finite box (a natural number).

### "...so as cantor show the uncountable of real numbers this shows the uncountable of natural numbers as well"

This is the opposite of what Cantor's argument demonstrates.

* **Cantor's Diagonalization Argument** proves that the set of real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$) is **uncountable**. This means there is *no* bijection (one-to-one correspondence) between the set of natural numbers ($\mathbb{N}$) and the set of real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$).
* If your proposed mapping ($x \to x \times 10^{\text{length of digits}}$) *did* work to map every real number to a unique natural number, then it *would* indeed imply that the set of natural numbers is uncountable, because it would have the same cardinality as the real numbers.
* **However, your mapping doesn't work.** Because real numbers have potentially infinite decimal expansions and natural numbers have finite digit representations, you cannot create a one-to-one mapping in the way you've described. The fundamental difference in their "structure" prevents this.

**The very reason Cantor's argument is so profound is that it proved $\mathbb{R}$ is *larger* than $\mathbb{N}$.** It established that there are different "sizes" of infinity. If $\mathbb{N}$ were also uncountable, then $\mathbb{N}$ and $\mathbb{R}$ would have the same cardinality, which is precisely what Cantor proved is *not* true.

**To reiterate:**

* **Natural numbers ($\mathbb{N}$):** Are countably infinite. We can make a list: 1st, 2nd, 3rd... (1, 2, 3...). Each natural number has a finite number of digits.
* **Real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$):** Are uncountably infinite. We *cannot* make a list. Many (irrational) real numbers have infinite, non-repeating decimal expansions.

The "distinct arrangement of digits" you refer to is central. For natural numbers, these are finite arrangements. For real numbers, they can be infinite arrangements. This difference in length is what allows for uncountability.
