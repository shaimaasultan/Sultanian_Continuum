You're trying to connect the algebraic shift of $s \rightarrow s+0.5$ with the real part of $s$ being $0.5$. Let's clarify how this works in complex numbers.

A complex number $s$ is generally written as $s = \sigma + it$, where $\sigma$ is the real part and $t$ is the imaginary part.

When we perform the substitution $s \rightarrow s+0.5$:
* The new variable is $s_{new} = (\sigma + 0.5) + it$.
* The **real part** of this new variable is $\sigma + 0.5$.
* The **imaginary part** remains $t$.

This means that adding $0.5$ to $s$ causes a **shift in the real part** of $s$ by $0.5$. It does not *force* the real part to become $0.5$. The real part becomes $0.5$ *only if* the original real part $\sigma$ was $0$.

The functional formula for $\zeta(s)$ (in its standard form) is inherently symmetric around the line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$. This is because it relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$.

When we derive a formula for $\zeta(s+0.5)$, we are essentially looking at the zeta function from a shifted perspective.
* If the original function $\zeta(s)$ has its critical line of symmetry at $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$,
* Then the function $\zeta(s+0.5)$ will have its corresponding line of symmetry at $\text{Re}(s) = 0$. (Because if you apply $s \rightarrow 1-s$ to the argument $s+0.5$, you get $(1-s)+0.5 = 1.5-s$. The midpoint between $s+0.5$ and $1.5-s$ is $(s+0.5 + 1.5-s)/2 = 2/2 = 1$. This is not the right way to think about symmetry lines of shifted functions).

Let's rephrase the symmetry:
* For $\zeta(s)$, the line of symmetry is $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$. This means if $s = 1/2 + \delta + it$, then $\zeta(s)$ is related to $\zeta(1/2 - \delta + it)$.
* When we consider $\zeta(s+0.5)$, we are evaluating the zeta function at a point $s' = s+0.5$. If $s' = \sigma' + it'$, then $\zeta(s')$ has symmetry about $\text{Re}(s') = 1/2$.
* Translating back to $s$: $s = s' - 0.5 = (\sigma' - 0.5) + it'$.
* So, if the original function $\zeta(s_{orig})$ has its zeros on $\text{Re}(s_{orig}) = 1/2$, then the zeros of $\zeta(s+0.5)$ would be at $s_{orig} - 0.5$. If $s_{orig} = 0.5+it$, then $s_{orig}-0.5 = it$. So the zeros of $\zeta(s+0.5)$ would lie on the imaginary axis ($\text{Re}(s)=0$).

Therefore, adding or subtracting $0.5$ to the variable $s$ does not *force* the real part to be $0.5$ for all values of $s$. It merely shifts the real part. Our derivations correctly show how the functional equation transforms under such a variable shift, consistently reflecting its inherent symmetry. However, this does not exclude the possibility of zeros existing off the $\text{Re}(s)=1/2$ line for the original $\zeta(s)$ function.
