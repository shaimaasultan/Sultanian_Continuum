You're zeroing in on a specific term, $2^s$, from the functional equation of the Riemann Zeta Function, and trying to connect it to the critical line at $Re(s) = 0.5$ via $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sin(\pi/4)$. This shows you're looking closely at the components, but there are a few misunderstandings in this connection.

Let's unpack your points:

1.  **The $2^s$ term in the functional equation:**
    The functional equation for the Riemann Zeta Function is indeed $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$.
    If $s$ is on the critical line, then its real part is $0.5$. So, if we consider $s = 0.5 + it$, then $2^s = 2^{0.5 + it} = 2^{0.5} \cdot 2^{it} = \sqrt{2} \cdot 2^{it}$.
    So, it's true that the factor of $\sqrt{2}$ arises from evaluating $2^s$ when $\text{Re}(s) = 0.5$.

2.  **$\sqrt{2}$ represented as $\sin(\pi/4)$ and "non-trivial zeros at 0.5":**
    * **$\sin(\pi/4)$:** This is where there's a mathematical inaccuracy. $\sin(\pi/4)$ (which is $\sin(45^\circ)$) is equal to $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, not $\sqrt{2}$.
    * **Connection to Zeros:** The appearance of $\sqrt{2}$ from $2^{0.5}$ (when $s=0.5+it$) is a property of *one part* of the functional equation. However, the critical line at $\text{Re}(s) = 0.5$ (where the non-trivial zeros are hypothesized to lie) arises from the **entire symmetry** of the functional equation, $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$, or equivalently the full expression:
        $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$.

    The critical line is where $s$ and $1-s$ are reflections of each other across the line $\text{Re}(s) = 0.5$. For example, if $s = 0.5 + it$, then $1-s = 1 - (0.5+it) = 0.5 - it$. Both are on the line $Re(s)=0.5$. The functional equation links the behavior of $\zeta(s)$ on one side of this line to its behavior on the other side. The zeros must therefore appear symmetrically around this line.

    The **location of the non-trivial zeros on the critical line is a consequence of this fundamental symmetry of the entire functional equation**, not just an isolated property of the $2^s$ term or any other individual factor in the equation. Each term ($2^s$, $\pi^{s-1}$, $\sin(\pi s/2)$, $\Gamma(1-s)$) plays a specific role in allowing the analytic continuation and establishing this symmetry.

**In essence:**

While your observation that $2^{0.5} = \sqrt{2}$ is mathematically correct, the connection you're trying to make from $\sqrt{2}$ to $\sin(\pi/4)$ (which is $\sqrt{2}/2$) is not accurate, and more importantly, the presence of $\sqrt{2}$ as part of $2^s$ when $Re(s)=0.5$ does not, by itself, "cause" the non-trivial zeros to be on the $Re(s)=0.5$ line. That property emerges from the interplay of all the components of the functional equation, which guarantees a deep symmetry for the zeta function around that specific line.
