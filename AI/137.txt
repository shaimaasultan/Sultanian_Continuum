You've brought up a very important distinction in mathematics, and it allows us to clarify what constitutes a proof versus a definition or a direct consequence.

1.  **Definitions:** A definition in mathematics sets the meaning of a term or a symbol. For example, the definition of the imaginary unit $i$ is that $i^2 = -1$. This is a foundational statement that we agree upon to build the system of complex numbers.

2.  **Properties derived from Definitions (like the cyclic pattern of powers of $i$):**
    The cyclic pattern of powers of $i$ ($i^1=i$, $i^2=-1$, $i^3=-i$, $i^4=1$, and then it repeats) is not itself a definition. It is a **direct and provable consequence** of the definition of $i$ and the rules of multiplication. You can rigorously demonstrate this pattern through simple algebraic steps:
    * $i^1 = i$
    * $i^2 = -1$ (by definition)
    * $i^3 = i^2 \cdot i = -1 \cdot i = -i$
    * $i^4 = i^3 \cdot i = -i \cdot i = -i^2 = -(-1) = 1$
    This pattern is fundamental and easily demonstrable from the definition.

3.  **Mathematical Identities and Theorems (like your "New Odd Identity" or the Riemann Hypothesis):**
    A mathematical identity or theorem is a statement that is *claimed to be true* and, as such, **requires a formal, rigorous proof** based on established definitions, axioms, and previously proven theorems.
    Your "New Odd Identity" is a new statement that relates certain mathematical concepts (odd numbers, angles, complex planes). While you may see a clear pattern, in mathematics, a pattern, no matter how compelling, is typically a **conjecture** or an **observation**. To elevate it to the status of a proven identity or theorem, a step-by-step logical argument (a proof) must be presented that demonstrates its truth under all applicable conditions.

    The demand for proof for a new identity is standard practice in mathematics, especially when it's connected to profound and unsolved problems like the Riemann Hypothesis. It's not about disbelieving your observations, but about verifying the logical soundness and universal applicability of the claim within the established framework of mathematics.
