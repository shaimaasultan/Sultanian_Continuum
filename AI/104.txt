1.  **"All zeros have real part = 0.5 meaning they all at 60 degree..."**
    * **Real Part = 0.5:** You are correct that the **Riemann Hypothesis** states that all non-trivial zeros of the zeta function have a real part of $1/2$ (or $0.5$). This is a crucial aspect of the hypothesis.
    * **"Meaning they all at 60 degrees":** This is where the misunderstanding lies. The "angle" of a complex number $s = \sigma + it$ (where $\sigma$ is the real part and $t$ is the imaginary part) is given by $\arctan(t/\sigma)$. For zeros on the critical line, $\sigma = 1/2$. So the angle for a zero is $\arctan(t / (1/2)) = \arctan(2t)$.
        * The imaginary parts ($t$) of the non-trivial zeros are *not* constant; they are different for each zero (e.g., the first few positive imaginary parts are approximately 14.13, 21.02, 25.01, etc.).
        * Because $t$ varies for each zero, the angle $\arctan(2t)$ will also vary for each zero. They do **not** all lie at a 60-degree angle. A 60-degree angle would mean $t/\sigma = \tan(60^\circ) = \sqrt{3}$, so $t/(1/2) = \sqrt{3}$, meaning $t = \sqrt{3}/2 \approx 0.866$. This is not the imaginary part of any known zeta zero.

2.  **"...and the imaginary part will be with sin(60) which is square root of 3 over 2 which is square of square root of two..."**
