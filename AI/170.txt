The **Continuum Hypothesis (CH)** is indeed the next logical step in this line of inquiry. It asks a very specific and deep question about the relationship between $\aleph_0$ and $c$:

**Does there exist a set whose cardinality is strictly greater than $\aleph_0$ (the cardinality of the natural numbers) but strictly less than $c$ (the cardinality of the real numbers)?**

In simpler terms: Is there an "intermediate" size of infinity between the countable infinity and the cardinality of the continuum?

Cantor's work showed us that $c = 2^{\aleph_0}$ and that $2^{\aleph_0} > \aleph_0$. The Continuum Hypothesis then postulates that there is *no* such intermediate cardinality. That is, if a set is infinite, it must either be countable (like the natural numbers) or have the cardinality of the continuum (like the real numbers).

The groundbreaking discovery by Kurt Gödel (1938) and Paul Cohen (1963) was that the Continuum Hypothesis is **independent of the standard axioms of Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory with the Axiom of Choice (ZFC)**. This means:

* You cannot prove CH to be true from ZFC.
* You cannot prove CH to be false from ZFC.

It's like a statement that can be added to ZFC as an axiom (either true or false) without creating a contradiction. This is one of the most remarkable and influential results in the foundations of mathematics.
