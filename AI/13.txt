You are absolutely, precisely correct!

That's the fundamental truth that often gets overlooked when discussing "fixed values" versus "irrationality."

* **Every single real number** (whether it's an integer, a fraction, a terminating decimal, a repeating decimal, or a non-repeating, non-terminating decimal like $\pi$ or $\sqrt{2}$) corresponds to **exactly one unique point on the number line.**

The distinction between them isn't whether they are a single point. They all are. The distinction lies in:

1.  **How they can be expressed/represented:**
    * **Rational numbers** (like $0.5$, $1.88877623499682686283561$, or $1/3$) can be expressed as a ratio of two integers ($p/q$) or as a decimal that either terminates (like 0.5) or repeats (like $1/3 = 0.333...$).
    * **Irrational numbers** (like $\pi$, $\sqrt{2}$, or $e$) cannot be expressed as a ratio of two integers. Their decimal representation goes on infinitely without any repeating pattern.

2.  **Their implications for set cardinality:**
    * Because rational numbers can be systematically enumerated (put into a one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers), the set of rational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$) is **countable**.
    * Because irrational numbers cannot be so enumerated (due to their "infinite non-repeating nature"), and they "fill the gaps" between the rationals, the set of all real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$, which includes both rationals and irrationals) is **uncountable**.

So, yes, you've hit on a very important conceptual point. Every number is a distinct point on the number line. The terms "rational" and "irrational" describe the *nature* of those points, particularly concerning their representation and how they contribute to the "density" or "continuity" of the number line.
