You're getting closer to a very subtle but important distinction in the realm of complex analysis.

It's not that **analytic continuation *did* create the $1/2$ in isolation.** Rather, the process of analytic continuation *revealed* or *established* the domain over which the Riemann Zeta Function exists and, in doing so, brought to light its **functional equation**.

Let's refine this:

1.  **Analytic Continuation (The Tool/Process):**
    This is the method used to extend the definition of a function (like the Riemann Zeta Function, initially defined by a series $\sum 1/n^s$ only for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$) to a larger domain in the complex plane, all while preserving its property of being analytic (complex differentiable).

2.  **The Functional Equation (The Property Revealed):**
    Once the Riemann Zeta Function is analytically continued to the entire complex plane (except for a simple pole at $s=1$), it is found to satisfy a remarkable identity known as its **functional equation**:
    $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$

3.  **The $1/2$ (The Symmetry Point):**
    The $1/2$ comes directly from the **symmetry of this functional equation**. Notice that the equation relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$.
    * If $s$ is on the line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$, let $s = 1/2 + it$.
    * Then $1-s = 1 - (1/2 + it) = 1/2 - it$.
    * Both $s$ and $1-s$ are equidistant from the real axis, and crucially, they are both on the line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$.
    * This means the functional equation expresses a profound symmetry of the zeta function around the vertical line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

**So, the sequence is:**

* **Analytic continuation** allows us to define the zeta function beyond its initial domain.
* Once defined on a larger domain, its **functional equation is discovered**.
* This functional equation, by its inherent structure, demonstrates a **symmetry around the line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$**.
* The **Riemann Hypothesis** then states that all *non-trivial* zeros of this function lie *exactly* on this axis of symmetry, $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

Therefore, the $1/2$ is not something that analytic continuation "did" in the sense of directly producing it as a number. Instead, analytic continuation made it possible to see the **entire function's structure**, which *includes* this fundamental symmetry around the line $Re(s) = 1/2$, which is then the subject of the Riemann Hypothesis regarding the zeros.
