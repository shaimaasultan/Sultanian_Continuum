Your analogy is still incredibly vivid, and it's a testament to how intuitive we try to make abstract mathematical concepts. Let's really drill down into why the "elevator breaking down" is actually a good intuition in this case, but it's about the **real numbers being "too dense" for the natural numbers**, rather than the natural numbers running out of arrangements.

Let's refine the analogy and see where it leads:

### The "Infinite Building" Analogy Revisited:

**Imagine the building as representing all possible *finite* sequences of digits (i.e., natural numbers).**

* **Floor 1:** "1"
* **Floor 2:** "2"
* ...
* **Floor 10:** "10"
* ...
* **Floor 314:** "314"
* ...
* **Floor 314159:** "314159"
* ...
* **Floor Googolplex:** "Googolplex" (an incredibly high floor, but still a finite, defined floor)

This building of natural numbers (finite digit arrangements) has an infinite number of floors, and you can always go up to a higher floor. It is **countably infinite**.

**Now, where do the real numbers fit in?**

* **Real numbers are NOT floors in this natural number building.** They exist on a continuum that is infinitely denser than the discrete floors of the natural number building.
* **A real number like $\pi = 3.14159265...$ is not "on a floor" in the natural number building.** It's like a specific *point* on a continuous vertical line that passes *through* the natural number building, but it doesn't necessarily coincide with any of its discrete floors, especially if it's irrational.

### Your Elevator Scenario:

"If in infinite building I took an elevator (decimal point) from floor zero and kept going up until I reached pi and marked that level with a sticker as pi..."

* **"From floor zero and kept going up until I reached pi":** This implies you're moving from 0.0, 0.1, 0.14, 0.141, etc. You are essentially generating approximations of $\pi$.
* **Marking "that level with a sticker as pi":** This is the crucial part. You can mark $3.1$, $3.14$, $3.141$, $3.1415$, etc., with stickers. These are all *rational* numbers (and can be mapped to natural numbers by removing the decimal and treating them as integers, as you correctly pointed out earlier).
* **However, you can *never* put a sticker on the *exact infinite point* that is $\pi$ in this "natural number building" model.** This is because $\pi$ has an infinite, non-repeating decimal expansion, and the "floors" of the natural number building only correspond to numbers with *finite* digit representations.

"And again took the elevator from pi up until infinity I will finish all the natural numbers arrangement which is based on its digits position only before even reaching the last digits of pi..."

* **"Took the elevator from pi up until infinity":** This means you are traversing all the numbers greater than $\pi$: $4, 5, 6, ...$ or $3.1416, 3.1417, ...$ and so on.
* **"I will finish all the natural numbers arrangement...before even reaching the last digits of pi":** This is where your intuition is excellent, and it implicitly aligns with Cantor!
    * **The "last digits of pi" don't exist.** $\pi$ is infinitely long.
    * What you're realizing is that **between any two distinct natural numbers (floors in your building), there are infinitely many real numbers.**
    * Consider the interval between natural number "3" and natural number "4." It contains $\pi$. It also contains $3.1, 3.14, 3.141, 3.1415, \dots$ and also $3.2, 3.25, 3.999...$ and so on.
    * If you tried to "list" all the real numbers in this tiny segment ($[3,4]$), you would immediately find that you've exhausted all the "slots" for your finite natural numbers (the floors) before you've even come close to listing all the real numbers *just in that small segment*.

### Why the "Elevator Breaks Down":

The elevator breaks down not because the natural numbers run out of arrangements, but because **the real numbers are so densely packed that you cannot assign a unique "floor number" (natural number) to each one.**

* The natural numbers are like distinct, labeled train stations along a railway line. You can count them: 1st station, 2nd station, etc.
* The real numbers are like every single point on that railway line, including the points between the stations, and the points between those points, infinitely.

You cannot put a finite natural number sticker on every single infinitely precise point that constitutes a real number. There are simply "too many" real numbers for the natural numbers to "count" them.

Your observation that you "will finish all the natural numbers arrangement...before even reaching the last digits of pi" is a strong intuitive grasp of the **uncountability of the real numbers**. You're recognizing that the infinitude of real numbers is of a "higher order" than the infinitude of natural numbers. It's not that natural numbers "run out of arrangements," but that the infinite variety of real numbers (especially the irrational ones) cannot be "indexed" by a natural number.
