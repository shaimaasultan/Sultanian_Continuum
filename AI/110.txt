Let's carefully differentiate the concepts again:

1.  **Your Algebraic Formulas (e.g., that simplify to $x$, $0.5x$, $x^2$, etc.):**
    * These are **algebraic identities** that hold for real numbers (with specific exclusions for points where denominators are zero).
    * The "simplification" you observe (e.g., factors of $y^2$ canceling out, or specific numerical factors like $\sqrt{2}$ and $1/2$ leading to $x$) is due to **multiplicative inverse properties and strategic algebraic construction**. It's about terms directly canceling each other out.
    * The "functional formula" aspect in your examples refers to the expression being a function of $x$ (and sometimes $y$), where its value changes with $x$.

2.  **The Riemann Zeta Function and its Functional Equation in Complex Analysis:**
    * This exists in the **complex plane**, involving complex variables $s = \sigma + it$.
    * The functional equation, $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$, is a deep **analytic identity**. It describes a fundamental symmetry and property of the zeta function.
    * **Analytic continuation** is the process of defining $\zeta(s)$ over the entire complex plane (beyond its initial sum definition for $\text{Re}(s)>1$) while preserving its complex differentiability (analyticity). This process **reveals** the functional equation.

Now, addressing your specific points:

* **"s=s-1 and s=s-0.5 as it is functional formula"**:

    * The idea of "s=s-0.5" in your formulas often translates to something like $x \rightarrow x-0.5$ (a shift of the input variable). This results in a horizontal translation of the graph of your algebraic identity (e.g., if $f(x)=x$, then $f(x-0.5)=x-0.5$ shifts the line). This is a simple transformation in basic algebra, not the complex analytical property of the zeta function.

* **"includes 2^s and not s-1 similar for sin"**:

It's the **interplay of the poles and zeros of the $\Gamma(1-s)$ and $\sin(\pi s/2)$ terms cancelling out the poles of $\zeta(1-s)$**, allowing $\zeta(s)$ to be defined everywhere else.
