Even if we repeat the operations s time when s is an image number

You're extending the idea of repeating the operation to a scenario where 's' itself is an imaginary number, or even a complex number.

It's crucial to distinguish between:
1.  **The variable $s$:** This is a complex number ($s = \sigma + it$), which can certainly be purely imaginary (if $\sigma = 0$). Functions like $\sin(s)$ or $\zeta(s)$ are defined for complex inputs.
2.  **The number of times an operation is repeated:** When we say we repeat an operation "N times," N must be a non-negative integer ($0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$). You cannot perform an operation a complex number of times (e.g., $i$ times or $0.5+2i$ times).

So, if we apply the substitution $s \rightarrow s+0.5$ for $N$ iterations (where $N$ is an integer), the argument of the sine function will indeed become $\sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2} + N \cdot \frac{\pi}{4}\right)$, regardless of whether $s$ itself is a real, imaginary, or complex number.

The output of the sine function will, of course, be a complex number if $s$ is complex. However, the "number of repetitions" is still an integer, not the value of $s$.

This expression can be expanded as $\sin\left(\frac{\pi i}{2} + \frac{\pi x}{4}\right)$.

This represents the sine term if the complex variable $s$ in our context were set up such that its imaginary part contributes to $\frac{\pi i}{2}$ and its real part contributes to $\frac{\pi x}{4}$.

To understand this complex sine function, we use the identity for sine of a complex number:
$\sin(A+iB) = \sin A \cosh B + i \cos A \sinh B$

In your expression, $A = \frac{\pi x}{4}$ and $B = \frac{\pi}{2}$.

So, expanding the sine term from your image:
$$\sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{4} + i\frac{\pi}{2}\right) = \sin\left(\frac{\pi x}{4}\right)\cosh\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) + i \cos\left(\frac{\pi x}{4}\right)\sinh\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)$$

This is the complex value of the sine term for the specified input, where $\cosh$ and $\sinh$ are the hyperbolic cosine and sine functions, respectively. The image you provided also shows this expansion and sample points for various `x` values.
