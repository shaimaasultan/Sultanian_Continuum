Yes, precisely. **Not every arrangement of digits without a decimal point is called a natural number.**

You've captured the essence of the distinction with your analogy: "like saying not every arrangement of letters is a word."

Let's elaborate:

1.  **Arrangement of Letters vs. Words:**
    * `a, p, p, l, e` is an arrangement of letters. It forms the word "apple."
    * `x, y, z, q, p` is an arrangement of letters. It does *not* form a word in English.
    * The rules of the English language (grammar, vocabulary) define which arrangements of letters constitute meaningful "words."

2.  **Arrangement of Digits vs. Natural Numbers:**
    * `3, 1, 4, 1, 5` is an arrangement of digits. When interpreted canonically, it forms the natural number `31415`. This is a *finite* sequence of digits.
    * `3, 1, 4, 1, 5, 9, 2, 6, 5, 3, 5, 8, 9, 7, 9, ...` (with the ellipsis indicating it continues infinitely) is an arrangement of digits. This *infinite* sequence of digits, even without a decimal point explicitly shown, does **not** form a natural number.

### The "Rules" that Define a Natural Number:

The "rules" (definitions) that make certain arrangements of digits natural numbers are:

* **Finiteness:** A natural number, when written in base 10 (or any base), must consist of a **finite** sequence of digits. There is a definite beginning and a definite end to the digits. No matter how large the number, you can always count how many digits it has.
* **Positional Value:** The digits have a value based on their position (ones place, tens place, hundreds place, etc.). This system inherently relies on a finite number of positions to define a finite value.
* **Axiomatic Definition:** Natural numbers are formally defined using axioms (like Peano Axioms or set-theoretic constructions) that establish them as discrete, finite quantities with a successor property. This formal definition inherently means they are finite strings of digits in any base representation.

### Why Infinite Strings are Different:

An infinite string of digits, like the expansion of $\pi$ (whether written $3.14159...$ or conceptually as $314159...$ without the decimal implying it's "at the end"), represents a **real number**.

* If the infinite string repeats (e.g., $0.333...$), it's a rational number.
* If the infinite string does not repeat (e.g., $\pi$), it's an irrational number.

Both rational and irrational numbers are part of the larger set of **real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$)**.

So, to explicitly answer your question:
Yes, exactly. The rules of mathematics define what constitutes a "natural number." Just like not all arrangements of letters form valid words, not all arrangements of digits form natural numbers. Specifically, infinite arrangements of digits fall outside the definition of natural numbers and instead describe real numbers.
