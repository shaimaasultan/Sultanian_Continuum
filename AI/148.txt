You've articulated a key part of the Riemann Hypothesis correctly: the hypothesis asserts that the real part ($a$) of all non-trivial zeros is $0.5$, without needing to find the exact imaginary part ($b$) for each one. That is precisely what a proof of the RH would establish.

However, the persistent misunderstanding lies in the phrase **"for all odds"**.

The Riemann Hypothesis is about **all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann Zeta function $\zeta(s)$**. These are specific **complex numbers**. They are *not* odd integers.

Demonstrating a property (like transforming to the imaginary axis) that holds true for **odd integers** (1, 3, 5, etc.) does **not** prove anything about the real part of these distinct **complex non-trivial zeros** of $\zeta(s)$. These are fundamentally different mathematical objects.

To prove the Riemann Hypothesis, one must show that for every single complex number $s = a+bi$ for which $\zeta(s)=0$ (excluding the trivial zeros), the value of $a$ *must* be $0.5$. A proof about the properties of odd integers cannot substitute for this direct proof about the complex zeros of the Zeta function.

You're asking why the real part of $0.5$ wouldn't hold for all non-trivial $\zeta(s)$ zeros, given that $\zeta(s)$ is linked to primes and primes are (mostly) odd. This question stems from a crucial misunderstanding of the values we are discussing.

Let's address your premise: **"Re(0.5) if it hold for 3,5, 7 , every odd"**.

This premise is **incorrect**.

* **For $s = 3$**: The real part of $s$ is $3$, not $0.5$. The value of $\zeta(3)$ is approximately $1.2020569...$, which is a real number, not zero.
* **For $s = 5$**: The real part of $s$ is $5$, not $0.5$. The value of $\zeta(5)$ is approximately $1.0369277...$, which is a real number, not zero.
* **For $s = 7$**: The real part of $s$ is $7$, not $0.5$. The value of $\zeta(7)$ is approximately $1.0083492...$, which is a real number, not zero.

In general, for any positive odd integer $s = 1, 3, 5, 7, \ldots$, the value of $\zeta(s)$ is a non-zero real number. These are not zeros of the zeta function, and their real part is certainly not $0.5$.

The **non-trivial zeros** of the Riemann Zeta function are specific **complex numbers** of the form $0.5 + it$ (if the Riemann Hypothesis is true), where $t$ is a non-zero real number. Examples are $0.5 + i(14.1347\ldots)$ and $0.5 + i(21.0220\ldots)$. These are not integers, and they are distinct from the odd numbers you mentioned.

The connection between $\zeta(s)$ and primes is indeed profound (via the Euler product). However, this connection does not mean that the **complex non-trivial zeros** are themselves prime numbers or odd integers. The properties of integers (odd or prime) do not directly transfer to constrain the real part of these complex zeros. The question of why their real part is $0.5$ (as conjectured) remains the central challenge of the Riemann Hypothesis.
