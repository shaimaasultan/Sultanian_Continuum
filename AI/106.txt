You are absolutely correct that **analytic continuation often involves much more profound transformations than just algebraic factoring and cancellation.** For certain functions, the extension of their domain involves relationships with fundamental mathematical functions like sine, cosine, and the Gamma function.

Let's focus on the **functional equation of the Riemann Zeta Function**, as this is precisely where these trigonometric and gamma function terms appear and facilitate its analytic continuation:

The completed Riemann Zeta function, often denoted $\xi(s)$, which is closely related to $\zeta(s)$, satisfies the functional equation:

$\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$

This equation exhibits a **symmetry around the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$**. If $s = \sigma + it$, then $1-s = (1-\sigma) - it$. If $\sigma = 1/2$, then $1-s = 1/2 - it$, which is the reflection of $s$ across the real axis, still on the critical line.

The more commonly stated form of the functional equation for $\zeta(s)$ itself is:

$\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$

Here's what those terms do in the context of analytic continuation:

1.  **$\Gamma(1-s)$ (Gamma Function):** The Gamma function is a generalization of the factorial function to complex numbers. It has simple poles at $s = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. In the functional equation, the $\Gamma(1-s)$ term handles the poles that arise when analytically continuing $\zeta(s)$ from $\text{Re}(s)>1$ to other parts of the complex plane. It also introduces poles at $1-s = 0, -1, -2, \ldots$, i.e., $s = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$.

2.  **$\sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right)$ (Sine Function):** This trigonometric term plays a crucial role:
    * It contributes to the "trivial zeros" of the zeta function at $s = -2, -4, -6, \ldots$. This is because $\sin(\frac{\pi s}{2})$ is zero at these negative even integers.
    * This term, along with the Gamma function, effectively "extends" the definition of $\zeta(s)$ to the left half of the complex plane (where $\text{Re}(s) < 0$), ensuring that the function remains analytic everywhere except for a simple pole at $s=1$.

**How this relates to "shifting" and "transforming to sin and cos":**

The functional equation **is** the "transformation" you're sensing. It's a deep identity that effectively allows $\zeta(s)$, initially defined by a sum only for $\text{Re}(s) > 1$, to be defined over the entire complex plane (except $s=1$). This "shift" from $s$ to $1-s$ on the right side of the equation, coupled with the behavior of the Gamma and sine functions, is what makes the analytic continuation possible. The $\sin$ function and $\Gamma$ function are not just arbitrary factors; they are essential components whose analytic properties (like poles and zeros) cancel out the "bad behavior" of other terms, allowing the function to be smoothly extended.

Your algebraic formulas, while cleverly designed to simplify through cancellation, involve operations on real numbers and simple algebraic identities. The "cancellation" of $y^2$ or constant factors like $0.5$ or $\sqrt{2}$ is a direct result of multiplicative inverses.

So, you are right that analytic continuation involves more than just factoring; it involves these powerful transcendental functions which "transform" the function in a precise way, allowing its definition to be extended while preserving its analytic properties.
