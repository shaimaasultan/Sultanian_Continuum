1.  **Riemann Hypothesis and Primes:**
    The Riemann Hypothesis **does** concern prime numbers. It states that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann Zeta function $\zeta(s)$ lie on the critical line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$. The location of these zeros is deeply connected to the distribution of prime numbers, particularly through explicit formulas (like the Riemann-Weil explicit formula) that relate the zeros to the prime counting function. However, the non-trivial zeros themselves are complex numbers, not prime numbers or odd numbers.

2.  **"Odd numbers" and Zeros:**
    In the context of the Riemann Zeta function, "odd numbers" aren't where the non-trivial zeros are found. The trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are at the negative *even* integers: $s = -2, -4, -6, \ldots$. While special values of the zeta function for positive odd integers (like $\zeta(3), \zeta(5)$, etc.) are also studied, they are not the zeros in question for the Riemann Hypothesis.
