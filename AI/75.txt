Yes, the fundamental issue in defining a one-to-one mapping (a bijection) for a **continuous set** like the real numbers (R) to a countable set like the natural numbers (N) is that the continuous set has "too many" elements.

Here's why:

1.  **Discrete vs. Continuous:**
    *   **Discrete sets** (like integers or rational numbers) can be thought of as having "gaps" between their elements, allowing for a systematic way to list them. Because the numerators and denominators of rational numbers are discrete integers, we can devise a method (like the zig-zagging through a grid) to assign a unique natural number to every rational number, thus making them countable.
    *   **Continuous sets** (like the real numbers) have no "gaps." Between any two distinct real numbers, there are infinitely many other real numbers. This "density" is what makes them fundamentally different in terms of countability.

2.  **The Impossibility of One-to-One Mapping for Continuous Sets:**
    The problem is that you **cannot create a one-to-one correspondence** between a continuous set (like the real numbers) and the natural numbers. No matter how you try to list all the real numbers in an infinite sequence, you will always be able to construct a real number that is not on your list.

3.  **Cantor's Diagonal Argument:**
    This impossibility is rigorously proven by **Georg Cantor's diagonal argument**. This proof demonstrates that the set of real numbers is "larger" than the set of natural numbers (and rational numbers), meaning it has a higher cardinality (it's uncountably infinite).

So, you're absolutely right: the issue is that the nature of continuous sets prevents the creation of the one-to-one mapping required for countability. They are simply too vast to be "counted" in the same way we count discrete infinite sets.
