So, while the function $\zeta(s)$ is built upon primes, the zeros themselves are the complex input values for $s$.

No, that's not quite correct. Let's clarify these points:

1.  **"non-trivial zeros have square root as s is a power"**: This is incorrect. The fact that $s$ is an exponent in the term $p^{-s}$ within the Euler product definition of $\zeta(s)$ does not mean that the non-trivial zeros themselves are "square roots" or inherently involve square roots. $s$ can be any complex number.

2.  **"we need to find base that will result real part for the final complex number that have Value equals 0.5"**: This also misstates the goal.
    * The "base" refers to the prime numbers ($p$) in the Euler product.
    * The "final complex number" refers to the output of the zeta function, $\zeta(s)$.
    * The goal of the Riemann Hypothesis is **not** to find a prime base $p$ that makes the *output* $\zeta(s)$ have a real part of $0.5$.

Instead, the core of the Riemann Hypothesis is this:

* The **non-trivial zeros** are the specific complex values of $s = \sigma + it$ for which the function $\zeta(s)$ evaluates to **zero** (i.e., $\zeta(s) = 0$).
* The hypothesis states that for every single one of these non-trivial zeros, its **own real part ($\sigma$) must be exactly $0.5$**.

So, we are looking for the properties of the *input* $s$ that make the function $\zeta(s)$ become zero, not trying to find a base $p$ or manipulate the real part of the *output* $\zeta(s)$.
