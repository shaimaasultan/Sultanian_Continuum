Let's address your statement and then discuss the diagram.

First, your statement: "real numbers are countable but irrational numbers are not."

This statement is **incorrect**.

*   The set of **Real Numbers (R)** is **uncountably infinite**. This means you cannot create a one-to-one correspondence between real numbers and the natural numbers; there are simply "too many" real numbers to list.
*   The set of **Irrational Numbers (I)** is also **uncountably infinite**. Since the real numbers (R) are the union of rational numbers (Q) and irrational numbers (I) (R = Q ∪ I), and we know Q is countably infinite while R is uncountably infinite, it logically follows that I must also be uncountably infinite. If I were countable, then R (the union of two countable sets) would also be countable, which contradicts the proven uncountability of R.

So, both real numbers and irrational numbers are uncountably infinite.

Now, regarding your diagram and why there isn't a nested circle for irrational numbers like N, Z, Q, and C:

The diagram you provided illustrates a hierarchy of number sets based on **subset relationships**:
*   **N** (Natural Numbers) is a subset of **Z** (Integers).
*   **Z** (Integers) is a subset of **Q** (Rational Numbers).
*   **Q** (Rational Numbers) is a subset of **R** (Real Numbers).
*   **R** (Real Numbers) is a subset of **C** (Complex Numbers).

This nested structure shows that every number in an inner set is also an element of the larger, outer set. For example, every natural number is also an integer, every integer is also a rational number, and so on.

The set of **Irrational Numbers (I)** is defined as the set of real numbers that are *not* rational. In set notation, `I = R \ Q` (Real Numbers minus Rational Numbers).

Here's why 'I' is not represented as a nested circle in the same way:

1.  **Not a Subset of Inner Sets:** Irrational numbers are not subsets of N, Z, or Q. In fact, irrational numbers and rational numbers are **disjoint sets**; they have no elements in common. An irrational number (like √2) is fundamentally different from any integer or rational number.

2.  **Complementary Relationship within R:** Instead of being a nested subset, the irrational numbers form the "rest" of the real numbers once the rational numbers are removed. If you were to draw a Venn diagram showing R, Q, and I, you would typically draw a large circle for R, a smaller circle for Q inside R, and then the region *within R but outside Q* would represent I.

Venn diagrams are used to show relationships like subsets, unions, and intersections.[1, 2, 3] The diagram correctly shows the subset relationships. The irrational numbers fit into the overall structure of real numbers, but not as a nested set in the same manner as N, Z, and Q.
