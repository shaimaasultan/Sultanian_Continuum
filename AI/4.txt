You've hit upon a very important distinction in mathematics between a **continuous function** and a **continuous set (or continuum)**.

Let's break down your statement:

1.  **"sin(x) is in Q"**: This is where the initial premise is incorrect.
    * The sine function, $\sin(x)$, takes any real number $x$ as input and outputs a real number in the range $[-1, 1]$.
    * While some values of $\sin(x)$ are rational (e.g., $\sin(0) = 0$, $\sin(\pi/6) = 1/2$, $\sin(\pi/2) = 1$), **many, many values of $\sin(x)$ are irrational.**
    * For example, $\sin(1)$ (where 1 is 1 radian) is an irrational number. $\sin(\sqrt{2})$ is also irrational. In fact, for most values of $x$, $\sin(x)$ is irrational.

2.  **"sin signal is consider continuous"**: This is **correct**. The sine function is a classic example of a continuous function. Its graph can be drawn without lifting your pen, and small changes in $x$ result in small changes in $\sin(x)$.

3.  **"then Q that include all sin values will be continuous as well"**: This conclusion is **incorrect**, primarily because of the first point (not all $\sin(x)$ values are rational), but also due to a fundamental misunderstanding of "continuous" in terms of sets.

Here's why:

* **The range of $\sin(x)$ is the interval $[-1, 1]$, which is an uncountable set of real numbers.** It includes both rational and irrational numbers.
* **The set of rational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$) is NOT continuous.** While $\mathbb{Q}$ is **dense** in $\mathbb{R}$ (meaning between any two distinct rational numbers, you can always find another rational number), it has "gaps" everywhere – these gaps are precisely the irrational numbers. Imagine trying to draw a line composed *only* of rational numbers; you couldn't, because it would be full of tiny, unfillable holes.
* **A continuous function maps from a continuous domain to a continuous range.** The continuity of the *function* $\sin(x)$ means that as $x$ varies smoothly, the *output* $\sin(x)$ also varies smoothly across the entire real number line from -1 to 1.
* If we were to hypothetically consider only the *rational* values that $\sin(x)$ can take (which is a subset of the actual values), this set of rational outputs would still be a subset of $\mathbb{Q}$. A subset of a non-continuous set does not become continuous simply because it's generated by a continuous function.

**In essence:**

The continuity of the $\sin(x)$ function refers to the smooth, gap-free progression of its output values over its entire real range $[-1, 1]$. This range includes both rational and irrational numbers, forming a true continuum.

The set of rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ is inherently "gappy" and not continuous, even though it's dense. Just because a continuous function can produce some rational numbers doesn't mean the collection of *all* rational numbers (or even just the rational outputs of that function) suddenly forms a continuous set.
