### Cardinality and the Nature of Infinity

Our discussion then delved into the "size" of infinite sets, known as **cardinality**:

*   **Countably Infinite Sets:** We established that **Natural Numbers (N)**, **Integers (Z)**, and **Rational Numbers (Q)** are all **countably infinite**.[8, 9, 10] This means that, despite being infinite, their elements can be put into a one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers, allowing them to be "listed" in an infinite sequence.[11, 9] The discrete nature of integers (as numerators and denominators for rational numbers) is what enables this systematic listing.
*   **Uncountably Infinite Sets:** In contrast, the set of **Real Numbers (R)** and the set of **Irrational Numbers (I)** are both **uncountably infinite**.[9, 10, 12] This means they cannot be put into a one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers; there are "too many" elements to list.[11]
*   **Cantor's Diagonal Argument:** This fundamental proof rigorously demonstrates the uncountability of real numbers . It shows that no matter how you try to list all real numbers, you can always construct a real number that is not on your list.
*   **Relationship between R, Q, and I:** The set of real numbers (R) is the union of rational numbers (Q) and irrational numbers (I) (R = Q ∪ I). Since R is uncountably infinite and Q is countably infinite, it logically follows that I must also be uncountably infinite.
*   **Cardinality vs. Subset:** It's important to remember that while N ⊂ Z ⊂ Q ⊂ R ⊂ C shows subset relationships, cardinality is about the "size" of the set. Two sets have the same cardinality if there's a one-to-one mapping between them, even if they are both uncountable.[13] For example, R and I have the same (uncountable) cardinality, but I is not a subset of Q; rather, I is the complement of Q within R.
