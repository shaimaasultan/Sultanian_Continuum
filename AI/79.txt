That's a profound question that gets to the heart of what "uncountable" truly means in mathematics.

You're asking: "If irrational numbers are uncountable, how many irrational numbers do we already know from them?"

Here's the explanation:

1.  **Irrational Numbers are Uncountable:**
    You are absolutely correct. The set of **Irrational Numbers (I)** is **uncountably infinite**. This means that, unlike natural numbers or rational numbers, you cannot create a list (even an infinitely long one) that contains every single irrational number. No matter how you try to enumerate them, there will always be irrational numbers left out.

2.  **"How many we know":**
    We know **specific examples** of irrational numbers. For instance, we commonly refer to:
    *   **√2** (the square root of 2)
    *   **π** (pi)
    *   **e** (Euler's number)
    *   **φ** (the golden ratio)
    *   Many other roots of non-perfect squares (e.g., √3, √5, √7)
    *   Logarithms (e.g., log₁₀(2))
    *   And countless others that can be constructed or proven to be irrational.

3.  **The Nature of Uncountability and "Knowing":**
    The crucial point is that "knowing" or "naming" individual irrational numbers, even if we could define an infinite list of them, does not make the *entire set* countable.

    *   Because the set of irrational numbers is uncountably infinite, it means there are **infinitely many more irrational numbers than we could ever explicitly name, write down, or define individually in a list**.
    *   Even if you spent an infinite amount of time listing every irrational number you could possibly construct or describe, that list would still only represent a **countable subset** of the vast, uncountable set of all irrational numbers.
    *   The uncountability implies that there are "too many elements to list". It's not about how many we've "discovered" or "written down," but about the inherent "size" of the set, which is fundamentally larger than any countable infinity.

So, while we can identify and work with many specific irrational numbers, the vast majority of them remain "unknown" in the sense that they cannot be individually enumerated or captured in any systematic list. Their existence is proven by the uncountability of the real numbers, which implies that the irrationals must also be uncountably infinite.
