That's a fascinating hypothetical scenario, and it leads to a direct contradiction in mathematics.

If we were to simultaneously accept both:
1.  **Georg Cantor's diagonal argument** (which proves that the set of **Real Numbers (R)** is **uncountably infinite**).
2.  The existence of a **one-to-one relation** (a bijection) between the set of **Irrational Numbers (I)** and the set of **Rational Numbers (Q)**.

Then we would arrive at a **mathematical impossibility**. Here's why:

*   **From Cantor's Diagonal Argument:** This argument rigorously demonstrates that the set of real numbers is "larger" than the set of natural numbers (and thus larger than the set of rational numbers, which are countably infinite). This means that the cardinality of R is strictly greater than the cardinality of Q.

*   **From the Relationship R = Q ∪ I:** The set of all real numbers (R) is composed of the union of rational numbers (Q) and irrational numbers (I).

*   **The Implication of a One-to-One Relation between I and Q:** If a one-to-one relation existed between I and Q, it would mean that the set of irrational numbers (I) has the same cardinality as the set of rational numbers (Q). Since Q is known to be **countably infinite**, this would imply that I is also countably infinite.

*   **The Contradiction:** If both Q and I were countably infinite, then their union, R (the set of all real numbers), would also be **countably infinite**. However, this directly contradicts the fundamental result of **Cantor's diagonal argument**, which proves that R is **uncountably infinite**.

Therefore, you cannot have both conditions simultaneously. The truth of **Cantor's diagonal argument** (that R is uncountably infinite) inherently means that the set of irrational numbers (I) **must also be uncountably infinite**. This uncountability of I is precisely what makes R uncountably infinite, preventing any one-to-one correspondence between I and Q.
