You're hitting on a very interesting point about the visualization and the simplification!

Let's re-examine the expression shown in the image:

$$\frac{1}{x - \frac{1}{x}} \cdot \left(\frac{x-1}{y}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{y}\right) \cdot y^2$$

And let's simplify it, just as we did before:

1.  **Simplify the first term:**
    As we've established, $\frac{1}{x - \frac{1}{x}} = \frac{x}{x^2 - 1}$ (assuming $x \neq 0, 1, -1$).

2.  **Simplify the product of the two middle terms:**
    $$\left(\frac{x-1}{y}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{y}\right) = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{y \cdot y} = \frac{x^2 - 1}{y^2}$$

3.  **Combine all the simplified parts:**
    $$\left(\frac{x}{x^2 - 1}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x^2 - 1}{y^2}\right) \cdot y^2$$

4.  **Perform the multiplication and cancel terms:**
    $$\frac{x \cdot (x^2 - 1) \cdot y^2}{(x^2 - 1) \cdot y^2}$$
    Assuming $x^2 - 1 \neq 0$ (so $x \neq 1, -1$) and $y^2 \neq 0$ (so $y \neq 0$), all the $(x^2 - 1)$ and $y^2$ terms cancel out, leaving:
    $$x$$

**Your observation is insightful and correct:**

* **Simplifies to `x`:** Yes, the expression simplifies to `x`. This means that for any valid input values of `x` (where $x \neq 0, 1, -1$) and `y` (where $y \neq 0$), the *output value* of the expression is simply `x`.

* **Works for any real number (with restrictions):** Indeed, this simplification holds true for any real numbers $x$ and $y$, as long as $x \neq 0, 1, -1$ and $y \neq 0$.

* **Dimensionality and the 3D plot:** This is the clever part of your observation.
    * The *expression itself* is a function of two variables, $f(x, y) = \frac{1}{x - \frac{1}{x}} \cdot \left(\frac{x-1}{y}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{y}\right) \cdot y^2$.
    * Therefore, to plot this function, you would typically need a 3D graph, where the $x$-axis and $y$-axis represent the input variables, and the $z$-axis (or $f(x,y)$ axis) represents the output value.
    * The 3D plot shown in the image, however, is a graph of $z = x$.
    * **This graph is a plane.** Specifically, it's a plane that is parallel to the $y$-axis and intersects the $xz$-plane along the line $z=x$. For any given $x$ value, the $z$ value is equal to that $x$ value, regardless of what $y$ is. This is why the graph looks like a slanted "wall" or "ramp" extending along the $y$-axis.

* **"still simplifying to on x which is the number line"**: This is conceptually accurate. Even though we introduced $y$ as a variable in the expression, due to the clever algebraic cancellation, $y$ effectively "disappears" from the final simplified form. The output value $z$ depends *only* on $x$. So, in a sense, the function's output still behaves like the simple line $z=x$, but this line is now "extruded" or "extended" infinitely along the $y$-axis in a 3D space.

So, yes, you've perfectly captured how an expression involving multiple variables can, through simplification, reveal a dependence on fewer variables, leading to interesting interpretations in higher dimensions. The 3D plot beautifully illustrates that the function's value only changes as $x$ changes, remaining constant along the $y$-direction for any given $x$.
