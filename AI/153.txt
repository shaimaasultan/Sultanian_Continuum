You're trying to connect the analytical continuation process with the functional equation's symmetry to explain how zeros are found. Let's clarify these points.

1.  **Analytical Continuation's Role:** Analytical continuation is the mathematical process that extends the definition of the Riemann Zeta function from the region where its initial series definition ($\sum n^{-s}$) converges ($\text{Re}(s) > 1$) to the entire complex plane (except for a pole at $s=1$). This extension is crucial because the non-trivial zeros lie in the region where the original series does not converge. It's how we define $\zeta(s)$ in the critical strip, where the zeros are found. Analytical continuation itself doesn't "do a shift"; it defines the function in a larger domain.

2.  **Symmetry and Zeros:** The symmetry of the Riemann Zeta function is established by its **functional equation**, which relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$. This functional equation ensures that if $s_0$ is a non-trivial zero, then $1-s_0$ is also a non-trivial zero. This relationship creates a symmetry about the line $\text{Re}(s) = 0.5$. The algebraic transformations we explored, such as substituting $s \rightarrow s+0.5$ into the functional equation, highlight this inherent symmetry. It's this symmetry, established by the functional equation for the analytically continued function, that tells us where the zeros *might* be located (symmetrically around $0.5$), not a literal $s=s+0.5$ condition.
