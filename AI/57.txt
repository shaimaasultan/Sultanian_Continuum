You're absolutely right that, mathematically, the set `{1, 2, 3, 4,...}` and the set `{1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0,...}` represent the **exact same collection of numbers**: the positive integers (or natural numbers). The `.0` after an integer does not change its numerical value.

The reason we typically see and use the notation `{1, 2, 3, 4,...}` (without the `.0`) is primarily due to:

1.  **Simplicity and Conciseness:** It's the most straightforward and least cluttered way to write integers. Why add extra characters (`.0`) if they don't convey additional mathematical information about the value itself?

2.  **Standard Mathematical Convention:** In number theory, algebra, and most branches of pure mathematics, integers are conventionally written without decimal points unless there's a specific reason to emphasize their nature as real numbers (e.g., when discussing precision or floating-point representation in computational contexts). The set `{1, 2, 3, 4,...}` is universally recognized as the set of natural numbers.

3.  **Distinction from Non-Integer Real Numbers:** While `1.0` is an integer, the notation `1.0` is more commonly associated with the broader set of **real numbers**, which includes numbers with non-zero decimal parts (like `1.5`, `2.73`, `π`, `√2`). By omitting the `.0`, we implicitly emphasize that we are dealing with discrete, whole values, rather than a continuous range or numbers that might have fractional components.

4.  **Historical and Foundational Context:** The natural numbers (and integers) are often introduced as fundamental building blocks before real numbers are fully explored. Their notation reflects this foundational simplicity.

So, while `{1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0,...}` is mathematically equivalent, `{1, 2, 3, 4,...}` is the standard, more concise, and less ambiguous notation when specifically referring to the set of positive integers. The "more general" set you refer to, `{1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0,...}`, doesn't actually contain any *more* numbers than `{1, 2, 3, 4,...}`. Both contain the same infinite sequence of whole numbers.
