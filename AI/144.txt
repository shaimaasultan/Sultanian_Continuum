Let's clarify:

1.  **Riemann Hypothesis and Primes/Odd Numbers:**
    The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) is indeed profoundly related to the **distribution of prime numbers**. Its proof would unlock deeper insights into how primes are spaced along the number line. However, the hypothesis itself is a statement about the **non-trivial zeros** of the Riemann Zeta function $\zeta(s)$. These non-trivial zeros are specific complex numbers, *not* integers (odd or prime). While there's a link to primes, the direct subject of the RH is the complex zeros.

2.  **Zeta Function Output and RH Claim:**
    You are correct that the output of the zeta function $\zeta(s)$ is a complex number, often written as $a+bi$. The Riemann Hypothesis claims that for all non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ (i.e., where $\zeta(s)=0$), the real part 'a' is exactly $0.5$. The hypothesis does *not* specify the value of the imaginary part 'b' for these zeros. Your understanding of this aspect of the RH is correct.

3.  **The Logical Disconnect: Odd Numbers and Non-trivial Zeros:**
    Here's the critical distinction:
    * Even if you can prove that **all odd numbers** (meaning the integers 1, 3, 5, 7, etc.) can be transformed to lie on the imaginary axis using your identity, this remarkable property of odd integers **does not directly or automatically translate to a proof about the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann Zeta function.**
    * The non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are fundamentally different mathematical objects from odd integers. They are complex numbers that arise from the analytic properties of the zeta function itself.
    * Showing that one set of numbers (odd integers) has a certain property after a transformation does not, by mathematical deduction, prove a property about a completely different set of numbers (the non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$).

To exclude the possibility of any non-trivial zeros lying outside the $\text{Re}(s)=0.5$ line, you must provide a rigorous proof that operates directly on the properties of the $\zeta(s)$ function itself, demonstrating that for any $s$ where $\text{Re}(s) \neq 0.5$ (and $s$ is not a trivial zero), $\zeta(s)$ is necessarily non-zero. The transformation of odd integers, however intriguing, doesn't establish this required connection.
