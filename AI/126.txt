I understand why you might draw that conclusion from the transformations we've been performing. It's true that the `0.5` is a recurring numerical value when we shift the variable $s$ in the functional equation.

However, it's important to distinguish between:
1.  **The statement of the Riemann Hypothesis.**
2.  **The algebraic manipulations we've performed on the functional equation.**

**What the Riemann Hypothesis (RH) actually states:**
The Riemann Hypothesis states that all *non-trivial zeros* of the Riemann Zeta Function $\zeta(s)$ lie on the **critical line** $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$. It is a conjecture about the specific location of these zeros in the complex plane. It does not state that zeros are "shifted" in the sense of appearing in a different location than their inherent positions.

**Our Algebraic Manipulations:**
What we've shown is that the functional equation, which describes the symmetry of $\zeta(s)$, can be expressed in different forms by shifting its argument.
* The original functional equation relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$.
* By substituting $s \rightarrow s+0.5$, we derived a functional equation for $\zeta(s+0.5)$, which relates it to $\zeta(0.5-s)$.

This consistent presence of the $0.5$ in the arguments (like $s+0.5$ and $0.5-s$) is indeed a consequence of the fundamental symmetry of the functional equation around the line $\text{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

**Regarding the "shift" of zeros:**
If the Riemann Hypothesis is true, then the non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are of the form $s_0 = 0.5 + it$ for some real number $t$.
* If we then look at the zeros of $\zeta(s+0.5)$, they would occur when $s+0.5 = s_0$, which means $s = s_0 - 0.5$. So, if $s_0 = 0.5 + it$, then $s = (0.5 + it) - 0.5 = it$. This means the zeros of $\zeta(s+0.5)$ would lie on the imaginary axis ($\text{Re}(s)=0$).
* Similarly, the zeros of $\zeta(s+1)$ would lie at $s_0 - 1 = (0.5 + it) - 1 = -0.5 + it$.

So, while shifting the *variable* $s$ by $0.5$ (or multiples thereof) does shift the *location* where the zeros of the *newly defined function* (like $\zeta(s+0.5)$ or $\zeta(s+1)$) would lie, it does not mean that the *original* zeros of $\zeta(s)$ are "shifted" in any way from their intrinsic positions.

The algebraic transformations we performed correctly demonstrate the re-parametrisation of the functional equation, which is built upon the inherent symmetry around the $1/2$ line. However, they do not constitute a proof or direct statement of the Riemann Hypothesis itself. The Riemann Hypothesis is specifically about the real part of the non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ being exactly $1/2$.
