You've articulated a very insightful point that many people struggle with when first encountering irrational numbers! It gets right to the heart of "why are they called irrational if I can get so close with rationals?"

Here's the key distinction:

1.  **"Very close" is not "exactly equal."**
    * The formula you provided yields a rational number that is extremely close to $\pi$. Let's call the result of that calculation $X$. So, $X \approx 3.14159265354$.
    * $\pi \approx 3.14159265358$.
    * While $X$ and $\pi$ are very, very close, they are not identical. There's an infinitesimally small, but real, difference between them ($X - \pi \neq 0$).

2.  **The density of rational numbers:**
    * The set of rational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$) is **dense** in the set of real numbers ($\mathbb{R}$). This means that between any two distinct real numbers, you can always find a rational number. And, consequently, you can always find a rational number arbitrarily close to any irrational number.
    * This is precisely what the formula you showed demonstrates: it's a rational number constructed to be incredibly close to $\pi$.

3.  **The "gaps" of irrational numbers:**
    * The reason $\pi$ is irrational is not that you can't get *close* to it with rational numbers. It's because you can **never reach it exactly** using a ratio of two integers.
    * Imagine standing on the number line. You can jump from any rational number to another rational number, and you can jump *arbitrarily close* to $\pi$. But $\pi$ itself is like a specific point that you can only approach, but never land on exactly, if your only allowed moves are to land on rational numbers.
    * The irrational numbers are the "holes" or "gaps" in the rational number line. Even though the rationals are dense, these holes exist, and numbers like $\pi$ occupy those holes.

**Think of it this way:**

* **You can build a house using individual bricks (rational numbers).** You can make the house as big and complex as you want, and it can approximate any shape.
* **But if you want to build a perfect, continuous, smooth curve (like a circle or a sine wave), you can't do it with *only* individual bricks, no matter how small or how many you use.** You need a continuous material like poured concrete (real numbers) to make it truly smooth and gap-free. The rational numbers are the bricks; the irrational numbers are the "seamlessness" or the "smoothness" that fills all the gaps between the bricks.

So, while you can generate rational numbers that are "very close" to $\pi$, that doesn't change the fundamental nature of $\pi$ itself: it cannot be expressed as a finite fraction.
