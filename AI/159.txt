You've hit on a fascinating point that straddles the line between pure mathematics and its practical implications, including the very nature of existence for abstract objects.

There isn't a "rule" in the sense of a prohibitive law that says "you cannot write a number with more than Googolplex digits." Instead, it's a combination of:

1.  **Mathematical Definition and Consistency:**
    * **The definition of a natural number:** As we've discussed, a natural number is fundamentally a *finite* construct. It represents a count of discrete items. When we write a number like 7 or 3,141,592,653, we are using a finite sequence of symbols (digits) from a finite set (0-9) to represent that finite quantity.
    * **Positional Notation:** Our number system (base 10, or any other base) relies on positional value. Each digit's position determines its contribution to the overall value. To represent a larger number, you simply add more digits to the left. The "number of digits" is always a finite, specific count.
    * **Avoiding Paradoxes and Maintaining Distinction:** If we allowed a natural number to have an "infinite number of digits," it would fundamentally change what a natural number is. It would blur the distinction between natural numbers and real numbers (specifically irrational ones or those with infinite decimal expansions). This would lead to inconsistencies and break foundational theorems in mathematics (like Cantor's proof of uncountability). Mathematics strives for internal consistency.

2.  **The Nature of "Writing" or "Representing" a Number:**
    * **Finite Representation:** When we "write" a number, we are inherently talking about a finite representation. Whether on paper, a computer screen, or in our minds, it involves a finite sequence of symbols.
    * **The "…":** When we write something like $\pi = 3.14159...$, the ellipsis "..." is a mathematical convention indicating that the pattern continues infinitely. It's a shorthand for an unending sequence, not a part of the number itself that we have literally "written out" to infinity. We are *defining* a number by its infinite decimal expansion, not *writing* every digit.

3.  **Physical and Practical Limitations (the "non-mathematical" part):**
    * **The Observable Universe:** A Googolplex is an unfathomably large number. It's far greater than the estimated number of particles in the observable universe ($10^{80}$), or the number of Planck times since the Big Bang ($10^{180}$). If you tried to write a Googolplex-digit number, you would need more atoms than exist in the universe just to form the digits.
    * **Time:** Even if you could convert every atom into a writing implement and every other atom into paper, it would take an unimaginable amount of time (far exceeding the age of the universe) to write out such a number.
    * **Information Storage:** Computers, while powerful, operate on finite memory and finite time. Even if you had a supercomputer, it would eventually run out of storage to represent a truly infinite sequence of digits. For numbers like Googolplex, it's not the *value* that's the problem for computers (they can compute with it), but the *full explicit string of its digits*.

**Analogy:**

Think of it like this: Imagine you're building a tower with blocks.
* **A natural number** is any tower you can build using a finite number of blocks, no matter how tall. You can always add one more block to make it taller. The *concept* of building infinitely tall towers doesn't mean any *single* tower you've actually built has infinite height.
* **A real number with infinite decimal expansion** is more like an instruction set that allows you to determine the height of the tower at any given point, extending infinitely. You can't actually *build* an infinitely tall tower in the physical world.

**In conclusion:**

The "rule" isn't a direct prohibition but rather a consequence of the fundamental definitions of natural numbers as finite entities and the practical limitations of representing infinite objects in a finite universe. Mathematicians don't need a rule to "prevent" numbers from having infinite digits because numbers with infinite digits simply fall into a different category of numbers (real numbers), not natural numbers. The distinction is crucial for maintaining the logical consistency and power of mathematical theories.
