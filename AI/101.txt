First, let's confirm the simplification of the new expression:

$$\frac{1}{x - \frac{1}{x}} \cdot \left(\frac{x-1}{2}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{2}\right) \cdot 4 \cdot 0.5$$

2.  We know that $\left(\frac{x-1}{2}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{2}\right) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{4}$.
3.  The remaining constants are $4 \cdot 0.5 = 2$.

So, combining these:
$$\left(\frac{x}{x^2 - 1}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x^2 - 1}{4}\right) \cdot 2$$

Assuming $x^2 - 1 \neq 0$ (i.e., $x \neq 1$ and $x \neq -1$):
$$\frac{x \cdot (x^2 - 1) \cdot 2}{(x^2 - 1) \cdot 4}$$
$$\frac{2x}{4} = \frac{1}{2}x = 0.5x$$

The simplification to $0.5x$ is correct.

Now, regarding your statement: "although the initial domain is not connected and have hole the final domain is connected for any X/2"

Let's clarify the terms:

1.  **Domain of the Original Function:**
    The original function is $f(x) = \frac{1}{x - \frac{1}{x}} \cdot \left(\frac{x-1}{2}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{x+1}{2}\right) \cdot 4 \cdot 0.5$.
    For $f(x)$ to be defined, its denominator cannot be zero. The problematic part is $x - \frac{1}{x}$.
    $x - \frac{1}{x} = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x}$.
    This expression is undefined if $x=0$.
    It is also zero if $x^2 - 1 = 0$, meaning $x=1$ or $x=-1$.
    Therefore, the **domain of the original function $f(x)$ is all real numbers EXCEPT $x=0, x=1, \text{ and } x=-1$.** This domain is indeed "not connected" because these three points are excluded.
    At these points, the graph of $f(x)$ will have **holes** (removable discontinuities).

2.  **Domain of the Simplified Expression:**
    The simplified expression is $g(x) = 0.5x$.
    The domain of this linear function is **all real numbers**. This domain *is* "connected."

3.  **Connecting the Two (Important Distinction):**
    The crucial point is that when you simplify $f(x)$ to $g(x)$, you are finding an expression that behaves identically to $f(x)$ **everywhere in the domain of $f(x)$**.
    However, the graph of the *original function* $f(x)$ still has the holes at $x=0, x=1, x=-1$. The simplified expression $g(x)$ (the straight line $y=0.5x$) *does not* have these holes.

    Think of it this way: The original function $f(x)$ is a specific function defined by its original formula and its domain. The simplified expression $g(x)$ is a *different* function, which happens to agree with $f(x)$ on $f(x)$'s domain. When mathematicians discuss "the function," they usually refer to the original definition, including its domain.

    So, you are correct that the *simplified expression* (which defines a new, simpler function) has a connected domain. But the *original function* itself still has the discontinuous domain due to the points where its original denominator was zero.

    The "for any X/2" part of your statement regarding "connectedness" doesn't change this. The division by 2 in the factors $\left(\frac{x-1}{2}\right)$ and $\left(\frac{x+1}{2}\right)$ involves a constant denominator, which never introduces new restrictions on $x$. The restrictions on the domain always come from variables in the denominator that could become zero.
