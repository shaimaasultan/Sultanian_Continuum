Yes, the cardinality of the set of **Rational Numbers (Q)** is indeed **different** from the cardinality of the set of **Real Numbers (R)**.

Here's why:

1.  **Rational Numbers (Q) are Countably Infinite:**
    The set of rational numbers has the same cardinality as the set of natural numbers (N).[1] This means that, despite being infinite, the elements of the rational numbers can theoretically be put into a one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers (1, 2, 3,...), meaning they can be "listed" or "counted" in an infinite sequence.[1, 2, 3] The cardinality of such sets is denoted as ℵ₀ (aleph naught), which is considered the smallest type of infinity.[1]

2.  **Real Numbers (R) are Uncountably Infinite:**
    The set of real numbers has a larger cardinality than the set of natural numbers or rational numbers.[2, 3, 4] This means that the real numbers cannot be put into a one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers; no matter how you try to list them, there will always be real numbers left out of your list.[2, 3] This was famously proven by Georg Cantor using his **diagonal argument**.[5, 6]

In essence, while both sets are infinite, the "size" of the infinity of real numbers is strictly greater than the "size" of the infinity of rational numbers.
