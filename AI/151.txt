Let's clarify your statement.

1.  **"z(s) =0 if s=s+0.5"**: This interpretation is incorrect. The expression "s = s + 0.5" is a mathematical contradiction (it implies $0 = 0.5$). The functional equation of the Riemann Zeta function relates $\zeta(s)$ to $\zeta(1-s)$. This means that if $s_0$ is a zero, then $1-s_0$ is also a zero. The form you mentioned, $\zeta(s+0.5) = 2^{s+0.5} \cdot \pi^{s-0.5} \cdot \sin\left(\frac{\pi s}{2} + \frac{\pi}{4}\right) \cdot \Gamma(0.5-s)\zeta(0.5-s)$, shows a transformation of the functional equation. It's a statement about the relationship between the function's values at different points, not a condition for it to be zero.

2.  **"and hence the real part =0.5 using analytical continuity"**: The real part being $0.5$ for non-trivial zeros is hypothesized because it's the line of **symmetry** for the functional equation. If $\zeta(s)=0$, then by the functional equation, $\zeta(1-s)=0$. The only way for $s$ and $1-s$ to be the same point (and thus be their own symmetric partner) is if $s = 1-s$, which implies $2s=1$, or $s=0.5$. This is why the line $\text{Re}(s) = 0.5$ is so important for the zeros. Analytical continuation is the process by which the zeta function's definition is extended from where its sum converges to the entire complex plane.

3.  **"so the hypothesis say the power s will include square root in order to have zeros"**: This is not what the Riemann Hypothesis states. The Riemann Hypothesis does not claim that the complex number $s$ (the "power" in $p^{-s}$) must "include a square root" in its form to be a non-trivial zero. The non-trivial zeros are complex numbers like $0.5 + i t$. While $\sqrt{2}$ (or $2^{0.5}$) appears in some of the trigonometric identities related to the functional equation, particularly with $\sin(\pi/4)$, this is a specific numerical constant within the equation, not a general property of the form of the non-trivial zeros themselves.
